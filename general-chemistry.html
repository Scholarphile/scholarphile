General Chemistry I

Explore the fundamental principles of chemistry including atomic structure, chemical bonding, stoichiometry, thermodynamics, and kinetics. Essential foundation for all chemistry and related sciences.

Chapter 1: Atomic Structure and Periodic Properties

1.1 Atomic Theory and Structure

The atom consists of a dense nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in orbitals.

Fundamental Particles

  • Proton: Charge = +1, Mass ≈ 1 amu, Location = nucleus
  • Neutron: Charge = 0, Mass ≈ 1 amu, Location = nucleus
  • Electron: Charge = -1, Mass ≈ 0.0005 amu, Location = orbitals

Atomic Number and Mass Number

$$\text{Atomic Number (Z)} = \text{Number of protons}$$ $$\text{Mass Number (A)} = \text{Number of protons + neutrons}$$ $$\text{Number of neutrons} = A - Z$$

Example: Carbon-14

Carbon-14 has 6 protons (atomic number) and mass number 14.

  • Protons: 6
  • Neutrons: 14 - 6 = 8
  • Electrons (neutral atom): 6

Isotope notation: $^{14}C$ or $^{14}C_6$

1.2 Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals according to specific rules:

  • Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: Maximum 2 electrons per orbital (opposite spins)
  • Hund's Rule: Fill orbitals singly before pairing

Orbital Order

1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d < 6p

Example: Electron Configurations

  • Carbon (Z=6): 1s² 2s² 2p²
  • Iron (Z=26): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶ or [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶
  • Chlorine (Z=17): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁵ or [Ne] 3s² 3p⁵

1.3 Periodic Trends

Atomic Radius

Decreases across a period (left to right), increases down a group

Ionization Energy

Energy required to remove an electron: $M(g) \rightarrow M^+(g) + e^-$

Increases across a period, decreases down a group

Electronegativity

Ability to attract electrons in a bond. Increases across a period, decreases down a group

Pauling scale: F (4.0) > O (3.5) > N (3.0) > Cl (3.0) > C (2.5)

Chapter 2: Chemical Bonding

2.1 Ionic Bonding

Formed between metals and nonmetals through electron transfer.

Lattice Energy

Energy required to separate an ionic solid into gaseous ions:

$$U \propto \frac{q_1 \cdot q_2}{r}$$

where $q_1$ and $q_2$ are ionic charges and $r$ is the distance between ions.

Example: Ionic Compound Formation

Formation of NaCl:

  • Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (ionization energy = +496 kJ/mol)
  • Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (electron affinity = -349 kJ/mol)
  • Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl(s) (lattice energy = -787 kJ/mol)

Overall: Na(s) + ½Cl₂(g) → NaCl(s) ΔH = -411 kJ/mol

2.2 Covalent Bonding

Formed between nonmetals through electron sharing.

Lewis Structures

Steps to draw Lewis structures:

  1. Count total valence electrons
  2. Determine central atom (usually least electronegative)
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds
  4. Complete octets with lone pairs
  5. Form multiple bonds if needed

Example: Lewis Structure of CO₂

Total valence electrons: C(4) + 2×O(6) = 16 electrons

Structure: O=C=O

Each oxygen has 2 lone pairs, carbon has no lone pairs

All atoms satisfy the octet rule

2.3 Molecular Geometry (VSEPR Theory)

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion theory predicts molecular shapes based on electron pair repulsion.

Common Geometries

  • Linear: 2 electron pairs (BeF₂, CO₂)
  • Trigonal planar: 3 electron pairs (BF₃)
  • Tetrahedral: 4 electron pairs (CH₄)
  • Trigonal bipyramidal: 5 electron pairs (PF₅)
  • Octahedral: 6 electron pairs (SF₆)

Example: Water (H₂O)

Central atom: O with 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs

Electron geometry: Tetrahedral

Molecular geometry: Bent (≈104.5° bond angle)

The lone pairs compress the H-O-H bond angle from 109.5° to 104.5°

2.4 Hybridization

Mixing of atomic orbitals to form hybrid orbitals for bonding.

  • sp³: Tetrahedral geometry (CH₄)
  • sp²: Trigonal planar geometry (C₂H₄)
  • sp: Linear geometry (C₂H₂)

Chapter 3: Stoichiometry and Chemical Equations

3.1 Balancing Chemical Equations

Conservation of mass requires equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides.

Example: Combustion of Propane

Unbalanced: C₃H₈ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Balanced: C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Check: C: 3=3 ✓, H: 8=8 ✓, O: 10=10 ✓

3.2 Mole Concept

The mole is the fundamental unit for counting particles in chemistry.

$$1 \text{ mol} = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ particles (Avogadro's number)}$$ $$\text{Moles} = \frac{\text{Mass (g)}}{\text{Molar mass (g/mol)}}$$

Example: Mole Calculations

How many molecules are in 18.0 g of water (H₂O)?

Molar mass of H₂O = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 g/mol

$$\text{Moles} = \frac{18.0 \text{ g}}{18.016 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.999 \text{ mol}$$
$$\text{Molecules} = 0.999 \text{ mol} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 6.01 \times 10^{23}$$

3.3 Limiting Reagent

The reactant that is completely consumed first, limiting the amount of product formed.

Example: Limiting Reagent Problem

2Al + 3Cl₂ → 2AlCl₃

Given: 5.4 g Al and 10.6 g Cl₂

Moles of Al = 5.4 g ÷ 26.98 g/mol = 0.20 mol

Moles of Cl₂ = 10.6 g ÷ 70.90 g/mol = 0.15 mol

From stoichiometry: 0.20 mol Al needs 0.30 mol Cl₂

We only have 0.15 mol Cl₂, so Cl₂ is the limiting reagent

Theoretical yield of AlCl₃: 0.15 mol Cl₂ × (2 mol AlCl₃/3 mol Cl₂) = 0.10 mol AlCl₃

Mass of AlCl₃ = 0.10 mol × 133.34 g/mol = 13.3 g

3.4 Percent Yield

$$\text{Percent Yield} = \frac{\text{Actual Yield}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}} \times 100\%$$

Chapter 4: Solutions and Concentration

4.1 Types of Solutions

  • Solvent: The dissolving medium (usually present in larger amount)
  • Solute: The dissolved substance
  • Aqueous solution: Water is the solvent

4.2 Concentration Units

Molarity (M)

$$\text{Molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{liters of solution}}$$

Molality (m)

$$\text{Molality} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{kg of solvent}}$$

Mass Percent

$$\text{Mass \%} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} \times 100\%$$

Example: Molarity Calculation

What is the molarity of a solution made by dissolving 58.5 g NaCl in enough water to make 500.0 mL of solution?

Molar mass of NaCl = 22.99 + 35.45 = 58.44 g/mol

Moles of NaCl = 58.5 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 1.00 mol

Volume = 500.0 mL = 0.5000 L

$$\text{Molarity} = \frac{1.00 \text{ mol}}{0.5000 \text{ L}} = 2.00 \text{ M}$$

4.3 Dilutions

When diluting solutions, moles of solute remain constant:

$$M_1V_1 = M_2V_2$$

Example: Dilution Problem

How much water must be added to 100.0 mL of 6.0 M HCl to make 2.0 M HCl?

$$M_1V_1 = M_2V_2$$ $$(6.0 \text{ M})(100.0 \text{ mL}) = (2.0 \text{ M})V_2$$ $$V_2 = 300.0 \text{ mL}$$

Water to add = 300.0 mL - 100.0 mL = 200.0 mL

Chapter 5: Acids and Bases

5.1 Definitions

Arrhenius Definition

  • Acid: Produces H⁺ ions in water
  • Base: Produces OH⁻ ions in water

Brønsted-Lowry Definition

  • Acid: Proton (H⁺) donor
  • Base: Proton (H⁺) acceptor

Example: Brønsted-Lowry Acid-Base Reaction

HCl + NH₃ → NH₄⁺ + Cl⁻

  • HCl is the acid (donates H⁺)
  • NH₃ is the base (accepts H⁺)
  • NH₄⁺ is the conjugate acid of NH₃
  • Cl⁻ is the conjugate base of HCl

5.2 pH and pOH

For aqueous solutions at 25°C:

$$K_w = [H^+][OH^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$$ $$pH = -\log[H^+]$$ $$pOH = -\log[OH^-]$$ $$pH + pOH = 14.00$$

Example: pH Calculation

Calculate the pH of 0.025 M HCl

HCl is a strong acid, so [H⁺] = 0.025 M

$$pH = -\log(0.025) = -\log(2.5 \times 10^{-2}) = 1.60$$

5.3 Strong vs. Weak Acids and Bases

Strong Acids (complete ionization)

HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄, HClO₃

Strong Bases (complete ionization)

Group 1 hydroxides (NaOH, KOH), some Group 2 hydroxides (Ba(OH)₂, Ca(OH)₂)

Weak Acids (partial ionization)

For weak acid HA: $HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-$

$$K_a = \frac{[H^+][A^-]}{[HA]}$$

Example: Weak Acid pH

Calculate the pH of 0.10 M acetic acid (CH₃COOH, Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵)

CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻

Let x = [H⁺] at equilibrium

$$K_a = \frac{x^2}{0.10 - x} = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$$

Assuming x << 0.10:

$$x^2 = (1.8 \times 10^{-5})(0.10) = 1.8 \times 10^{-6}$$ $$x = 1.3 \times 10^{-3} \text{ M}$$ $$pH = -\log(1.3 \times 10^{-3}) = 2.89$$

Chapter 6: Thermodynamics

6.1 First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or converted.

$$\Delta U = q + w$$

where ΔU = change in internal energy, q = heat, w = work

6.2 Enthalpy (H)

Enthalpy is the heat content of a system at constant pressure.

$$\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(PV) = q_p \text{ (at constant pressure)}$$

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH°f)

The enthalpy change when 1 mole of compound is formed from its elements in standard states.

By definition: ΔH°f = 0 for elements in their standard states

Example: Calculating ΔH°rxn

Calculate ΔH° for: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

Given ΔH°f values (kJ/mol):

  • CH₄(g): -74.8
  • CO₂(g): -393.5
  • H₂O(l): -285.8
  • O₂(g): 0
$$\Delta H°_{rxn} = \sum \Delta H°_{f,products} - \sum \Delta H°_{f,reactants}$$
$$= [(-393.5) + 2(-285.8)] - [(-74.8) + 2(0)]$$ $$= -965.1 - (-74.8) = -890.3 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

6.3 Entropy (S)

Entropy is a measure of the randomness or disorder of a system.

  • ΔS > 0: Increase in disorder (favored)
  • ΔS < 0: Decrease in disorder (unfavored)

6.4 Gibbs Free Energy (G)

Gibbs free energy determines spontaneity of reactions.

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$
  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous reaction
  • ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous reaction
  • ΔG = 0: Equilibrium

Example: Spontaneity Prediction

For a reaction at 298 K: ΔH = -125 kJ/mol, ΔS = -85 J/mol·K

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$ $$= -125,000 \text{ J/mol} - (298 \text{ K})(-85 \text{ J/mol·K})$$ $$= -125,000 + 25,330 = -99,670 \text{ J/mol} = -99.7 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

Since ΔG < 0, the reaction is spontaneous at 298 K.

Chapter 7: Chemical Kinetics

7.1 Reaction Rates

The rate of reaction is the change in concentration over time.

$$\text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{d[B]}{dt} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{d[C]}{dt} = \frac{1}{d}\frac{d[D]}{dt}$$

for the reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD

7.2 Rate Laws

The rate law expresses the relationship between rate and concentration:

$$\text{Rate} = k[A]^m[B]^n$$

where k = rate constant, m and n = reaction orders

Example: Determining Rate Law

For the reaction A + B → C, given experimental data:

Trial [A] (M) [B] (M) Rate (M/s)
1 0.10 0.10 2.0 × 10⁻³
2 0.20 0.10 8.0 × 10⁻³
3 0.10 0.30 2.0 × 10⁻³

Comparing trials 1 and 2: [A] doubles, rate increases 4×, so order in A = 2

Comparing trials 1 and 3: [B] triples, rate unchanged, so order in B = 0

Rate law: Rate = k[A]²

k = (2.0 × 10⁻³)/((0.10)²) = 0.20 M⁻¹s⁻¹

7.3 Integrated Rate Laws

Zero Order: Rate = k

$$[A]_t = [A]_0 - kt$$ $$t_{1/2} = \frac{[A]_0}{2k}$$

First Order: Rate = k[A]

$$\ln[A]_t = \ln[A]_0 - kt$$ $$t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{k}$$

Second Order: Rate = k[A]²

$$\frac{1}{[A]_t} = \frac{1}{[A]_0} + kt$$ $$t_{1/2} = \frac{1}{k[A]_0}$$

7.4 Activation Energy and Temperature

The Arrhenius equation relates rate constant to temperature:

$$k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}$$ $$\ln k = \ln A - \frac{E_a}{RT}$$

where Ea = activation energy, A = pre-exponential factor, R = gas constant

Chapter 8: Chemical Equilibrium

8.1 Equilibrium Constant

For the reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

$$K_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$$
  • K >> 1: Products favored
  • K << 1: Reactants favored
  • K ≈ 1: Neither side strongly favored

Example: Equilibrium Expression

For: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)

$$K_c = \frac{[NH_3]^2}{[N_2][H_2]^3}$$

8.2 ICE Tables

Initial, Change, Equilibrium tables help solve equilibrium problems.

Example: ICE Table Problem

For H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), Kc = 54.3 at 700K

Initial concentrations: [H₂] = 0.10 M, [I₂] = 0.20 M, [HI] = 0 M

H₂ I₂ HI
I 0.10 0.20 0
C -x -x +2x
E 0.10-x 0.20-x 2x
$$K_c = \frac{[HI]^2}{[H_2][I_2]} = \frac{(2x)^2}{(0.10-x)(0.20-x)} = 54.3$$

Solving this quadratic equation gives x = 0.078 M

Equilibrium concentrations: [H₂] = 0.022 M, [I₂] = 0.122 M, [HI] = 0.156 M

8.3 Le Châtelier's Principle

When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to counteract the disturbance.

  • Concentration changes: Adding reactant shifts right, adding product shifts left
  • Temperature changes: Increasing T favors endothermic direction
  • Pressure changes: Increasing P favors side with fewer gas molecules
  • Catalysts: Increase rate but don't affect equilibrium position
